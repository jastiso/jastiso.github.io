Jennifer Stiso

Currently, I'm a research engineer in the Complex Systems Group at theUniversity of Pennsylvania, working with Prof. Danielle Bassett. I got my PhD in neuroscience from Penn working with Prof. Danielle Bassett and Prof. Timothy Lucas in 2021, and I graduated the University of California Berkeley with a B.A. in Molecular and Cellular Biology, and Cognitive Science in 2016. At Berkeley, I worked in the lab of Prof. Robert Knight.

In my PhD, I studied network models of perturbations to neural dynamics. I found combining computational modeling with time-resolved neural recordings (usually intracranial EEG) to be an especially useful, interesting, and fun way to investigate these topics. My research now extends to network models of human learning and interaction. As a research engineer, I also spend a lot my time doing software engineering tasks for the Complex Systems Group to help other people's research go smoothly.

How do we understand other people? Frontiers for Young Minds, (2016). Imagine this: you walk into class and see your friend sitting alone at a table. You notice your friend is looking downward, with a frown on her face. You would probably think from these clues that your friend is sad. But how did you know that? One way that your brain accomplishes this is by simulating or copying in your mind what you see the other person doing. This may help you understand that when you are frowning and looking downward, you are usually sad, so it is probably the case that your friend is sad too. While there are other hypotheses for how our brain understands others, we are going to focus on simulation. We will first examine neuroimaging experiments, in monkeys and in humans, in which we use technology to get an indication of brain activity. These brain activations help us understand simulation better.
